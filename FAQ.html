What is Anthony Nolan?

You must be aged 16-39 and meet our medical criteria (general good health).

Once I've given a sample, is that it?

This is the first step towards potentially saving someones life. If you come up as a possible match we'll contact you to explain what happens next.

iI'm 31 and healthy. Why can't I join the register?

Donors between the ages of 16-30 are far more likely to be chosen by hospitals to go ahead for transplant. We have limited funds and resources, so we need to focus on recruiting people who have the best possible chance of being chosen to donate.

If you'd like to help our work in some other way, we'd be incredibly grateful. For more information, visit anthonynolan.org/8-ways

Is the procedure painful?

90% of our donors now give their stem cells via the bloodstream. It's a simple, painless process, very similar to giving blood.

If you give your bone marrow, you'll be under anaesthetic. Afterwards you'll probably feel a bit tired and bruised, and you might have to take a little time off work. That's all.

Donating through blood

Nearly 90% of all of our donations are done this way

1. Have some tests

You go to a specialised hospital and have some tests done - blood tests, a chest x-ray, that sort of thing. A week later, we'll call to say the tests are fine, and then send you details about things like dates, hotels and transportation.

2. Daily injections

You'll receive a four day course of injections that stimulate your body to produce stem cells. A nurse comes to your office or home to give you the injections. You'll probably notice your bones aching a little. Don't worry, its totally normal.

3. Go to the hospital

You and your companion will then head to the hotel where you'll stay the night - don't worry, we pay for it all. The next morning, you'll head over to the hospital.

4. The big day

Now you're ready to donate. You're connected to a machine which takes blood from one arm, filters out the stem cells, and returns your blood to the other arm. The process takes four to five hours. It can get a bit boring, so you might want to watch telly or chat to your companion. If there are enough stem cells - and there usually are - you can go home. If not we need you to have another injection and return the next day for another session on the machine.

5. Get some rest

After your big day you'll probably feel tired and might experience some flu-like symptoms. These always pass after a few days. Get plenty of well-deserved rest and you'll soon be feeling right as rain

6. The difference it makes

Your stem cells are the things which are missing from the person you're a match for. Yours will kick-start there system into action and help them recover from a very vulnerable state. You've potentially saved a life. Amazing, huh?

Donating through bone marrow

This is unlikely, but it's sometimes better for the patient

1. Have some tests

As with donating by blood, You go to a specialised hospital for some simple tests, we'll call to say the tests are fine, and then send you details about things like dates, hotels and transportation.

2. Go to the hospital

You travel with your companion to the hospital. You stay in the hospital and your companion stays in a hotel. Don't worry about the cost - we'll pay for it all.

3. The big day

In the morning, you'll be given a general anaesthetic. During a short procedure - about 30 minutes - you'll lie on your front and bone marrow is taken from your pelvic bone using a needle and syringe. You won't need stitches after the procedure but you'll have two plasters where the bone marrow was taken from. People think donating bone marrow is really painful, but donors tell us that it's not any worse than how you'd feel after a heavy workout in the gym

4. Stay overnight

You wake up and you might feel a bit bruised where the needles were inserted. Your companion arrives and you spend the day and one more night in hospital recovering from the anaesthetic before going home the next day.

5. Get plenty of rest

For the next few days you'll probably feel some pain where the needles were inserted, as well as general tiredness. Take some paracetamol and get some well-deserved rest. You'll soon be right as rain.

6. The difference it makes

The stem cells you've donated will be used to give someone with blood cancer that most special gift - the chance of life. Well done! That makes you pretty fantastic.
